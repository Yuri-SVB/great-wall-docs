\section{Conclusion}
% ════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The Deadly Race turns ``graceful degradation'' from a virtue into a liability
whenever the degraded state is a feasible-but-slower path to the secret: by
Lemma~\ref{lem:drl}, that state is a race, and the race pays an assassination
incentive. The No-Gray-Area Criterion is the resulting design bar, and the
reachability corollary shows there are exactly two honest ways to clear it. The
TGPO orbit clears it by removing the race entirely --- a $\sftstar$ floor
from every seizable state (Theorem~\ref{thm:floor}) --- and so keeps custody
individual, whereas time-lock-rescue vaults clear the same bar by removing the
attacker's reach at the cost of a trusted third party.

\paragraph{What devicelessness buys: a threat model that stays simple.}
Beyond clearing the bar, keeping the whole setup in the head keeps a series of
otherwise-open cans of worms firmly shut --- and it is this that lets the model
above stay \emph{a fortiori} and single-shot rather than a tangle of sub-games. The
tacit, deviceless TGPO holder enjoys:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=1.9em]
  \item a \textbf{minimal window} in which the stash $K$ is seizable at all --- the
        bounded residual (\S\ref{sec:residual}), not a standing device \emph{(the
        funds survive)};
  \item a \emph{separate} \textbf{minimal window} of deadly-race exposure \emph{(the
        owner survives)} --- distinct from~(1), and only mostly overlapping it: once
        the second-to-last point of stage $N$ is surrendered, the stash is effectively
        lost while the deadly-race window is at its \emph{peak} (an impatient
        $\Attacker$, or one perceiving a threat to its escape, may still kill);
  \item \textbf{no hostage device} --- and so none of the game-theoretic bluffing
        maze of ransoming a seized token (\S\ref{sub:hostage});
  \item an NNPP that is a \textbf{dead end}: $\Attacker$ may assume an undisclosed
        shortcut device exists (Assumption~\ref{lem:nnpl}), but killing $\Victim$
        cannot extract it, and $\Attacker$ knows $\Victim$ has no \emph{need} of one
        --- so denial is inherently plausible (Remark~\ref{rem:plausible-denial});
  \item \textbf{no trusted third party} (route \ref{r:removerace}, not
        \ref{r:remotewinner});
  \item \textbf{maximum portability} --- no geographic binding to sites of custody
        (Definition~\ref{def:geographic});
  \item the \textbf{lowest operational cost} of the three routes.
\end{enumerate}
Each is a simplification that device-, geography-, or delegate-dependent designs
forfeit; taken together they are why the deviceless secret admits so plain a threat
model.

\paragraph{Open items for the full draft.}
The impossibility chain is drafted here (\S\ref{sec:chain}); the remaining work
is: an \emph{explicit adversary game} --- $\Attacker$ a PPT
adversary augmented by a bounded physical-coercion oracle --- making
Criterion~\ref{prin:nga} a theorem rather than a stipulated bar; a
utility model richer than the single-shot $(1 - p_A)\,W > c$ (repeated
encounters, partial extraction, heir cascades, $\Attacker$'s risk-aversion); the
$\sftstar$-floor \emph{reduction} discharging \S\ref{sec:gw}'s $p_A = 0$
obligation; a quantitative $\Hhard$ per-evaluation calibration tying \ref{s:feasible}/\ref{s:infeasible} to
hardware; and an empirical grounding of the residual-window argument against the
wrench-attack registry~\cite{lopp-attacks}.
